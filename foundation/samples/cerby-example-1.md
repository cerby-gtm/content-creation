## **IGA deployments cost more than the contract says**

What percent of your apps are actually governed? If you’re like most enterprise orgs, the answer is probably about a third. 

A THIRD. 

Meanwhile, the costs associated with that are inversely proportional \- you end up paying about 3x the license cost.

### **The 3x rule in IGA deployment costs**

A $700,000 license means $2.1 million in deployment. That's before maintenance, connector add-ons, or the GSI's next SOW. In reality, it ends up closer to $3 million. And the platform still doesn't govern most of the app estate.

Cloud licenses might cost less, but cloud deployments don't. Phase one runs at least twice the annual subscription fee, no matter the architecture.

Phase one covers about 30 apps.

* The SOX-critical systems with working connectors.   
* HR for joiner-mover-leaver.   
* The access reviews compliance needs for the next audit. 

A typical enterprise has hundreds more apps that need coverage.

### **The connector model is why implementations stall**

After phase one, the GSI works the next tier for another year \- but every app tends to hit the same wall.

* The connector misses an edge case.  
* The vendor's API changed.  
* The legacy system needs custom integration from scratch.

More money gets poured into GSI hours while coverage velocity slows.

And then there are the internal setbacks.

* New leadership  
* Budget runs out  
* Someone decides the GSI isn't delivering

When either of these happen, the program is at risk of resetting. That might mean a completely new vendor altogether, resetting and restarting the entire program back to app one.

Pre-built connectors exist for the big platforms, but the comprehensive ones cost extra. That means something like SAP becomes an add-on, not a base feature. Everything else, the GSI builds on top of a generic API or database connector. Once it's built, the IGA vendor isn’t responsible for maintaining it while the GSI probably doesn't support it. When it breaks, you pay to rebuild it.

### **Everything outside IGA runs on spreadsheets**

60 to 70 percent of the app estate never makes phase one, so compliance teams build a workaround. In regulated organizations, it usually looks like this:

→ Compliance flags the SOX-regulated apps with no IGA connectivity.

→ App owners get two weeks to export entitlements and access rights to Excel.

→ Junior consultants build access reviews from the exports.

The reviews get “done”, and the audit passes. But next year? Same drill \- bigger fire.

That's what partial coverage looks like financially. Every app outside the platform adds overhead with more consultant hours and more audit exposure.

Not to mention partial coverage is what leads to access being incorrectly gained, managed or worse: kept. Our 2025 Identity Automation Gap Report found that 58% of teams report former employees retained access after leaving. That’s what unreviewed access piling up between cycles starts to look like.

### **Three-year deployments outlast the CISOs who approve them**

The average tenure of a CISO is 18 months ( {insert source here} )

But a CISO signing for IGA is committing the company to a three-year minimum before meaningful coverage.

If CISO tenure runs shorter than the deployment, accountability breaks. The executive on the hook isn't in the seat when it finishes. Whoever inherits it has different priorities and a different read on the original vendor pick. What looks like a failed deployment is often a leadership change.

Speed isn't just operational. A program that hits real coverage in 12 to 18 months fits one executive's tenure. A program that takes three years to reach 30 percent outlasts the person who approved it.

### **Reaching the apps your IGA connectors can’t**

The three-year timeline and the partial coverage ceiling aren't laws of physics. They're what happens when the whole program runs on connector development. When the connector is the only path to an app, the connector backlog sets the pace for everything.

Cerby reaches the apps the connector model can't. Joiner-mover-leaver runs automatically. Sessions terminate on demand. Access reviews run continuously, not annually. Entitlements data flows into the IGA platform in the format it already consumes, so certification campaigns finally include the apps that used to live in Excel. New integrations go live in 7 to 10 days. The apps that would have been phase four (if phase four ever got funded) become reachable without another multi-year GSI engagement.

This isn't a replacement for IGA. IGA governs what it governs. Cerby covers what IGA doesn’t reach. Together, coverage moves from 30 or 40 percent to something compliance can defend and the next CISO can build on.
