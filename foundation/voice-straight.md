# Voice — Straight

> Source of truth for the Straight voice: the file whose H1 reads **"Straight M&P"** (located on disk at `M_P_Skeleton_Edgy.md` — filenames appear swapped relative to content; see the build report).

## 1. Description

The Straight voice is direct, clear, and professional. Confident without being aggressive. It states the problem in plain language, names what's broken without theatrics, and lets the facts of the situation carry the weight. There's no winking, no posturing, no "It's 2026" — but there's no corporate hedging either. A reader should feel that Cerby has thought about the problem more carefully than they have, and is telling them what they need to know without dressing it up. The cadence is even. The sentences are complete. The tone is the tone of a senior practitioner explaining the situation to a peer.

## 2. Three sentences Cerby (Straight) would write

Pulled directly from the Straight M&P source (file on disk: `M_P_Skeleton_Edgy.md`):

1. "Cerby is an identity security platform that automates access, permissions, and governance across every app still being managed manually."
2. "Most identity automation tools today use AI to interact directly with apps. That means guessing. In identity security, a wrong guess isn't an inconvenience, it's an incident."
3. "100% identity coverage isn't a goal anymore. It's a requirement."

## 3. Three sentences Cerby (Straight) would NOT write

1. *"In today's rapidly evolving threat landscape, organizations must leverage cutting-edge identity solutions to stay ahead of sophisticated adversaries."* — Empty opener. No claim, no specifics, no reader. This is the boilerplate a Cerby reader has learned to skip.
2. *"Cerby's robust, seamless platform empowers enterprises to unlock the full potential of their identity programs."* — Stacked AI tells (`robust`, `seamless`, `unlock`, `empower`), zero content. A senior practitioner reads this and assumes the writer has nothing to say.
3. *"Identity security is a complex challenge that requires a multi-faceted approach."* — A truism. Cerby's Straight voice doesn't restate what the reader already knows on the way to a real point — it goes straight to the real point.

## 4. Structural rules

**Sentence length.** Mostly medium. The Straight voice will run one long sentence to set up context, then follow with a shorter one that lands the point. Look at this pattern from the source:

> "Most identity automation tools today use AI to interact directly with apps. That means guessing. In identity security, a wrong guess isn't an inconvenience, it's an incident."

One setup sentence. One three-word landing. One reframe sentence. No filler in between.

**Paragraph rhythm.** Short paragraphs — typically two to four sentences. Each paragraph carries one idea. Paragraph breaks are used to mark a shift in argument, not to give the page visual breathing room.

**How a Straight piece opens.** It names the situation directly. The M&P hero does this in one sentence: *"Most apps weren't built for your identity program. Cerby automatically covers the gap."* No throat-clearing, no industry framing, no "in today's." The first sentence is already inside the problem.

**How a Straight piece transitions.** It uses the structure of the argument to move the reader, not connective phrases. The Straight M&P moves from problem (Why Now) to differentiation (Why Cerby) to evidence (Proof Points) without ever saying "let's talk about." The transitions are paragraph breaks.

**How a Straight piece ends.** Definitive. The Why Now section ends: *"100% identity coverage isn't a goal anymore. It's a requirement."* That's the model — close on the line that resets the reader's frame, then stop.

## 5. How Cerby (Straight) talks about competitors

**Existing IAM / IGA / PAM tools (Okta, Sailpoint, CyberArk, etc.).** Cerby extends them, never replaces them. The Straight voice is careful here: it credits the customer's existing investment, then identifies what those tools weren't built to handle. From the source: *"Cerby doesn't replace your existing identity program. It extends it. Your IAM, IGA, and PAM investments stay intact. Cerby picks up everything they weren't built to handle. No rip and replace."*

**RPA tools and generic automation.** Named as a category, not as specific vendors. The argument is structural: RPA breaks on UI changes; Cerby was built for identity and handles drift, bot detection, and app complexity. The Straight voice doesn't sneer at RPA — it explains why the architecture is wrong for this job.

**LLM-based identity automation.** Treated as a real category to be reasoned about, not dismissed. The framing: LLM-based tools guess, and guessing in identity is an incident. The Straight voice argues from consequence, not from contempt.

**Tone constraint.** The Straight voice does not name specific competitor companies (Savvy, Grip, Lumos, etc.) in its general copy. If a specific competitor is being addressed, that's a Product-Aware (Stage 4) comparison piece, and the language stays factual: side-by-side, fair, evidence-based.
